# How to DP Shorts With Images

DP planning for a Short is not about making every shot cinematic.

It is about making every shot shootable.

In this example, Maya starts bored, tired, and low on energy. She decides to do a 30-second exercise burst, moves through jumping jacks, high knees, and squats, then ends energized. The storyboard tells us the emotional change. The DP plan tells us how the camera captures that change without losing the action.

Use this guide with [How to Storyboard Shorts With Images](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images), [How to Light Shorts With Images](/blog/lighting-shorts-with-images), and the creator case study [Why Making Something Meaningful Is Hard](/blog/meaningful-short-video-ai).

## What a DP sheet should decide

A useful DP sheet turns a creative frame into physical instructions.

It should answer:

- Where is the camera?
- What is the shot size?
- What lens or phone mode is used?
- Is the camera static or moving?
- Where does the actor stand or move?
- What must stay inside the 9:16 safe frame?
- What should be checked before rolling?

For Shorts, safe framing matters more than fancy movement. If the hands, feet, face, or action are cropped at the wrong moment, the viewer loses the point of the shot.

## Shot 1: DP the tired hook

![Shot 1 DP sheet: camera setup for Maya's tired close-up](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-1.jpg)

The first shot needs closeness.

Maya is low energy, so the camera should be close enough for the face, eyes, and hand gesture to do the work. A wide shot would make the emotion feel distant.

DP notes:

- Eye-level camera keeps the moment human.
- Close-up or medium close-up keeps attention on the face.
- Static framing lets fatigue feel still.
- Background should stay quiet and uncluttered.

The DP choice supports the hook: the viewer should recognize the mood instantly.

## Shot 2: DP the validation beat

![Shot 2 DP sheet: direct-address camera plan for Maya seeking validation](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-2.jpg)

Shot 2 is still a face-led moment, but the purpose changes.

Now Maya is asking the viewer if they feel the same. The DP plan should keep the camera stable and direct. Do not add movement just because the Short needs energy later. This beat works because it feels like a real person talking to the viewer.

Good DP decisions here:

- Keep the lens around mobile 1x wide for a natural phone look.
- Hold the eyeline near the lens.
- Keep headroom controlled.
- Let the posture stay slightly slumped so the next beat has contrast.

The camera is not performing yet. Maya is.

## Shot 3: DP the decision moment

![Shot 3 DP sheet: wider frame for Maya's 30-second power-up challenge](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-3.jpg)

Shot 3 is where the frame opens up.

Maya stands, takes control, and introduces the power-up challenge. The DP plan needs enough room for posture, hands, and a clear body line.

This is a common Short mistake: creators keep the same tight talking-head frame after the story has become physical. The viewer hears "let's move," but cannot see the body make the decision.

Use a wider frame when:

- The body language changes.
- The creator physically commits to an action.
- The next shot needs room for movement.
- The viewer needs to see confidence, not just hear it.

Shot size should follow the beat.

## Shot 4: DP the first exercise burst

![Shot 4 DP sheet: full-body camera plan for jumping jacks](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-4.jpg)

Jumping jacks are a safe-zone test.

The camera must be far enough back to hold the full body, arms, and feet inside the vertical frame. A slightly boring static camera is better than an energetic camera that crops the action.

Before rolling, check:

- Hands visible at the widest arm position.
- Feet visible at the widest step.
- Face still readable.
- No furniture in the movement path.
- Camera does not need to chase the action.

For solo creators, this is where a quick rehearsal saves the shot. Record five seconds, watch the edges of the frame, then adjust.

## Shot 5: DP the mid-workout push

![Shot 5 DP sheet: full-body camera plan for high knees](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-5.jpg)

High knees move vertically, so the DP plan protects headroom and footroom.

The sheet keeps Maya centered with the camera fixed. That makes the effort easy to read. The viewer can see the raised knee, arm drive, and face at the same time.

DP priorities:

- Keep Maya centered on the movement axis.
- Leave enough headroom for bounce.
- Keep the phone at eye level or slightly lower only if form still reads.
- Avoid handheld shake unless the style intentionally calls for it.

The movement creates the energy. The camera should make that energy legible.

## Shot 6: DP the final exercise push

![Shot 6 DP sheet: camera plan for squats and visible form](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-6.jpg)

Squats need form clarity.

The camera should show the full body so the viewer understands the action and sees the effort. If the frame is too tight, the shot becomes just another expression shot. If it is too wide, the payoff in her face gets lost.

This is the DP balance:

- Full body for form.
- Face readable for emotion.
- Static camera for control.
- Enough floor visible to understand movement.

In a Short, exercise shots should still tell story. This shot says Maya is working, but the work is starting to become energy.

## Shot 7: DP the payoff

![Shot 7 DP sheet: medium close-up payoff with Maya energized and confident](/blog/storyboard-shorts-with-images/dp-shot-7.jpg)

The final shot comes back closer.

That return matters. The Short began as a close emotional moment, and now it ends as a close emotional answer. Maya is upright, energized, and ready for the CTA.

DP notes for the ending:

- Medium close-up keeps face and posture visible.
- Eye-level camera restores direct connection.
- Clean background keeps attention on the change.
- The frame should feel brighter and more open than Shot 1.

The ending works because the camera compares two states: the tired close-up and the charged close-up.

## DP checklist for Shorts

Use this before shooting any vertical Short:

- Mark the actor's starting position.
- Check headroom, footroom, and handroom.
- Decide if the camera should move or stay static.
- Match shot size to the story beat.
- Record a five-second test for action shots.
- Watch the edges of the frame, not just the face.
- Keep the final shot visually connected to the first shot.

The best DP plan for a creator Short is usually simple. It lets the viewer understand the action without feeling the camera struggle.
