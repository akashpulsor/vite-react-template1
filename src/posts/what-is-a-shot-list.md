# What Is a Shot List?

![Shot list framework](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/framework.webp)

## Introduction

A shot list is a production plan that tells you exactly what to record before you pick up the camera. It turns an idea, script, outline, or story beat into a practical list of shots: what the camera sees, what action happens, what sound matters, and why the shot exists.

That last part matters most.

Many creators think a shot list is only a film-school document for large crews. In reality, a shot list is even more useful when you are shooting alone. There is no assistant director reminding you that you forgot the close-up. There is no cinematographer checking whether the wide shot and insert will cut together. There is no editor on set asking whether the footage will make sense later.

The shot list becomes that second brain.

It protects the idea from shoot-day chaos. Instead of recording whatever happens to be in front of you, you record what the viewer needs to understand. That is the difference between footage and a video.

This guide explains what a shot list is, why it matters, what to include, how to create one, how it works with storyboards, and how to use it for short-form videos, talking-head content, product demos, interviews, and creator shoots.

## What a Shot List Is

A shot list is a list or table of every shot needed to make a video work. Each row describes one shot. The row can be simple or detailed depending on the project.

At minimum, a useful shot list should include:

- Shot number.
- Framing or shot size.
- Action.
- Subject.
- Audio or dialogue note.
- Purpose of the shot.

For a larger shoot, it may also include:

- Scene number.
- Location.
- Camera angle.
- Camera movement.
- Lens.
- Equipment.
- Props.
- Lighting notes.
- Estimated setup time.
- Storyboard frame.
- Continuity notes.
- Shoot status.

The shot list is not the same thing as a script. The script tells you what happens or what is said. The shot list tells you how to capture it.

It is also not exactly the same thing as a storyboard. A storyboard shows the shot visually. A shot list organizes the shot logistically. They work best together: the storyboard helps you see the frame, and the shot list helps you execute it.

Here is a simple creator shot list:

| Shot | Framing | Action | Sound | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Medium front | Creator points at messy desk | Voice | Name the problem |
| 2 | Close-up | Hand moves clutter away | Natural sound | Show the fix beginning |
| 3 | Overhead | Notebook opens to one task | Pen scratch | Make the idea concrete |
| 4 | Wide desk | Creator starts recording | Room tone | Show the new setup |
| 5 | Split compare | Before and after desk frame | Voice | Prove the result |

That is enough for many short-form videos. It gives the shoot direction without burying the creator in production paperwork.

The most important column is purpose. A shot should not exist only because it looks nice. It should reveal context, show proof, create emotion, clarify action, cover an edit, or help the viewer follow the idea.

## Why a Shot List Matters

Shot lists matter because shooting creates pressure. Once the camera is on, you are thinking about performance, sound, lighting, framing, timing, memory, props, background, and whether the take felt good. It is easy to miss a simple shot that would have saved the edit.

The edit is where missing shots become expensive.

You may discover that the hook has no visual proof. The product demo has no close-up. The recipe has no final plate. The interview answer has no cutaway. The fitness clip has no shot showing form. The talking-head video has no B-roll to cover a jump cut. The before-and-after video has the before but not the after.

A shot list prevents that.

It also makes filming faster. When each shot has a job, you stop wandering around the scene looking for ideas. You know what to capture first, what can be skipped if time runs out, and what must be recorded before the setup changes.

For teams, a shot list creates alignment:

- The director knows the visual plan.
- The camera operator knows framing and movement.
- The producer sees what affects time and budget.
- The editor gets the coverage needed to build the cut.
- The client or reviewer understands what will be captured.

For solo creators, the shot list reduces mental load. You do not need to remember every insert, angle, or proof shot while also performing. You can simply work down the list.

Shot lists do not remove creativity. They protect it. When the basics are planned, you have more room to notice better options on set. The list gives you a plan to change from, not a cage.

## Step-by-Step Process to Create a Shot List

![Shot list process](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/process.webp)

### Step 1: Start with the point of the video

Do not start with camera angles. Start with the viewer promise.

Write one sentence:

- "This video shows how to make a desk video look cleaner."
- "This Short explains why phone footage looks flat."
- "This product demo shows how to turn one long recording into a Short."
- "This interview clip explains one mistake founders make when hiring editors."

The point of the video tells you what shots are necessary. If the video is about improving desk lighting, you need a before shot, the light position, the movement, and the result. If the video is about a product workflow, you need screen proof. If it is about an interview insight, you need the speaker, enough context, and possibly a reaction or caption setup.

### Step 2: Break the idea into beats

A beat is a moment of meaning. It can be a problem, action, reveal, example, result, or transition.

For a short video, your beats might be:

- Problem.
- Cause.
- Fix.
- Proof.
- Rule.

For a product demo:

- Pain.
- Current manual step.
- Product action.
- Result.
- Next step.

For an interview clip:

- Question or setup.
- Claim.
- Example.
- Lesson.
- Ending line.

The shot list comes from these beats. Do not list random visuals. Decide what the viewer needs to understand, then choose the shot that communicates each beat clearly.

### Step 3: Choose the shot size for each beat

Shot size controls how close the viewer feels to the subject.

| Shot size | Best use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Wide shot | Shows location, body, setup, or full action | Creator at desk with light and camera visible |
| Medium shot | Shows person and context | Talking-head explanation |
| Close-up | Shows emotion, object, detail, or proof | Hand moving the light |
| Insert | Shows a specific item or action | Phone screen, notebook, product button |
| Overhead | Shows layout or process | Desk setup, recipe, packing, sketch |
| Screen shot | Shows software or digital workflow | Product demo or tutorial |

A common mistake is using close-ups because they feel cinematic. Close-ups are useful when the detail matters. If the viewer needs the full setup, start wider. If the viewer needs proof, move closer.

### Step 4: Add action, not just nouns

Bad shot lists use vague nouns:

- "Desk."
- "Coffee."
- "B-roll."
- "Laptop."

Better shot lists describe action:

- "Creator pushes clutter outside the frame."
- "Coffee stays untouched while the laptop opens."
- "Cursor moves from raw video to final Short."
- "Hand turns the light away from the camera."

Action gives the editor something to cut around. It also gives the viewer something to follow.

### Step 5: Add the purpose of the shot

The purpose column tells you why the shot exists.

Examples:

- Show problem.
- Reveal current setup.
- Explain the fix.
- Prove the result.
- Cover jump cut.
- Show product action.
- Create emotional pause.
- Establish location.
- Preserve context.

If you cannot write the purpose, the shot may not be needed. This is how a shot list prevents overshooting.

### Step 6: Add sound and dialogue notes

Creators often plan visuals and forget sound. A shot list should include audio when sound affects the edit.

Add notes like:

- Voiceover continues.
- Natural click sound.
- Clean dialogue needed.
- Capture room tone.
- Music starts after this shot.
- Do not cover this line with B-roll.
- Keep the question before the answer.

For talking-head videos, sound is the main asset. For product demos, voice and screen action must match. For interviews, the question may be required to preserve meaning. The shot list should protect those needs.

### Step 7: Plan coverage only where it helps the edit

Coverage means capturing multiple angles or shot sizes so the editor has options. It can include a wide shot, medium shot, close-up, insert, reaction, and cutaway.

Coverage is useful when:

- The action is complex.
- The edit needs pacing options.
- The speaker may stumble.
- The scene needs emotional rhythm.
- The video needs before-and-after proof.
- You need a clean way to hide cuts.

Coverage is wasteful when it adds shots that do not help the idea. For a simple Short, one strong shot per beat may be enough. For a longer scene, coverage protects the edit.

### Step 8: Put shots in shooting order

Narrative order is the order the viewer sees. Shooting order is the order that saves time.

If shots 1, 4, and 6 all use the same camera angle, shoot them together. If shots 2 and 5 need the overhead setup, shoot them together. If the lighting changes for the final result, capture all "before" shots before you move the light.

For small creator shoots, this can save a lot of time. For team shoots, it helps schedule equipment, location, lighting, talent, and setup changes.

Add a simple setup label:

| Setup | Shots |
|---|---|
| A: Talking head front | 1, 6 |
| B: Wide desk | 2, 4 |
| C: Overhead | 3 |
| D: Split compare | 5 |

This turns the shot list into a shoot-day plan.

### Step 9: Mark priority

Not every shot has equal value. Mark each shot as must-have, useful, or optional.

- Must-have: the video fails without it.
- Useful: improves clarity or pacing.
- Optional: nice if time allows.

This protects the shoot when time runs out. If you only have 20 minutes, capture the must-have shots first. Optional B-roll can wait.

### Step 10: Review the list like an editor

Before recording, read the shot list and imagine the edit.

Ask:

- Can the viewer understand the problem?
- Is there a clear action or change?
- Is there proof of the result?
- Do I have enough context?
- Do I have a shot to cover awkward cuts?
- Is the sound plan clear?
- Can this be shot in the time available?

If the answer is no, fix the list before filming. It is much cheaper to add a row than to reshoot after editing.

The best way to understand a shot list is to see how weak notes become useful production instructions.

| Weak note | Useful shot list row |
|---|---|
| Desk shot | Wide desk shot: messy desk, laptop closed, creator hesitates. Purpose: show the problem before the fix. |
| Close-up | Close-up of hand moving the light 30 degrees sideways. Purpose: show the exact action. |
| B-roll | Insert of phone screen showing bad framing. Purpose: prove why the video looks amateur. |
| Product screen | Screen recording of upload, AI-selected moment, and final Short preview. Purpose: show product workflow. |
| Interview clip | Medium shot of guest answering after question is heard. Purpose: preserve context. |

### Example: 30-second creator Short

Short idea: "Your desk video looks flat because your light is too close to the camera."

| Shot | Framing | Action | Sound | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Talking head | Creator points to flat face lighting | Voice | Name the problem |
| 2 | Wide desk | Show light beside camera | Voice | Reveal current setup |
| 3 | Overhead diagram | Draw camera, face, light | Pen sound | Explain the move |
| 4 | Wide desk | Move light 30 degrees sideways | Soft click | Show action |
| 5 | Split compare | Before and after face lighting | Voice | Prove result |
| 6 | Talking head | Creator gives one rule | Voice clear | Make it memorable |

This can be filmed quickly because every shot has a job. If shot 3 is missing, the explanation may feel abstract. If shot 5 is missing, the result may not feel proven.

### Example: product demo Short

Video idea: "Turn one long recording into a finished Short."

| Shot | Framing | Action | Sound | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Screen close-up | Long video file appears | Voice | Show source material |
| 2 | Screen recording | Topic and story are selected | Voice | Show setup |
| 3 | UI close-up | AI-generated storyboard appears | Click | Show planning output |
| 4 | Vertical preview | Final Short structure is visible | Voice | Show what to record |
| 5 | Talking head | Creator says the next step is filming | Voice | Make the action clear |

This kind of shot list is useful because it connects planning to production. The creator does not stare at a blank timeline. They see what needs to be captured.

When you create a shot list, capture screenshots or references that make the plan visible:

- Storyboard frame.

- Final vertical preview.

Screenshots help when you return to the shoot later. They also help collaborators understand the frame before production starts.

## Common Mistakes

![Professional shot list workflow](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/advanced.webp)

The first mistake is being too vague. "Close-up of product" is not enough. What part of the product? What action? What does the viewer need to understand?

The second mistake is listing shots without story purpose. A shot list full of pretty visuals can still fail if the video has no clear progression.

The third mistake is overshooting. More shots can create more options, but they can also slow the shoot and bury the editor. Capture enough coverage to protect the edit, not every angle you can imagine.

The fourth mistake is ignoring setup time. If one shot needs a new light, tripod position, screen recording, prop, or background, group similar shots together. Do not rebuild the same setup three times.

The fifth mistake is forgetting sound. A beautiful shot with unusable audio may not help the final video. Include voice, natural sound, silence, room tone, and music notes when they matter.

The sixth mistake is treating the shot list as permanent. The list should guide the shoot, but better ideas may appear on set. Make changes deliberately. If you drop a shot, know what purpose you are dropping with it.

The seventh mistake is not thinking about the edit. A shot list should include cutaways, inserts, reaction shots, or proof shots when the edit needs them. Do not leave the editor with only one angle and no way to tighten the pacing.

## Tools and Templates

A shot list can be made in a notebook, spreadsheet, Notion table, Google Sheet, production board, or dedicated video planning tool. Dalaillama can also create an AI-driven shot list and storyboard from your topic and story, so you can see the shots before you record. The tool matters less than whether the plan gives you the right fields.

Use this basic template:

| Field | What to write |
|---|---|
| Shot number | 1, 2, 3 or 1A, 1B, 1C |
| Setup | Talking head, overhead, screen recording, product table |
| Framing | Wide, medium, close-up, insert, overhead, screen |
| Action | What happens in the shot |
| Audio | Voice, dialogue, natural sound, silence, music note |
| Purpose | Why the shot exists |
| Priority | Must-have, useful, optional |
| Status | Planned, shot, needs reshoot, approved |

For short-form creators, keep the template lean:

- Hook shot.
- Problem shot.
- Proof shot.
- Action shot.
- Result shot.
- CTA or final rule.

For product videos, add:

- Screen area.
- Cursor action.
- UI state.
- Zoom note.
- Caption note.

For interviews, add:

- Question.
- Answer timestamp.
- Context needed.
- Cutaway or reaction.
- Claim check.

For cinematic or narrative shoots, add:

- Scene number.
- Lens.
- Camera movement.
- Blocking.
- Lighting note.
- Estimated setup time.

The template should match the production. A solo creator does not need a 20-column film table. A larger shoot should not rely on memory and a few vague notes.

## FAQ

### What is a shot list?

A shot list is a planning document that lists the shots needed for a video or scene. It describes what the camera captures, what action happens, what sound matters, and why each shot exists.

### What should be included in a shot list?

Include shot number, framing, action, subject, audio note, purpose, priority, and status. Larger productions may also include scene number, location, lens, equipment, movement, lighting, setup time, and storyboard frames.

### What is the difference between a shot list and a storyboard?

A storyboard shows what the shot should look like. A shot list organizes what must be captured and how the shoot should run. They work well together: visual planning plus practical execution.

### Do solo creators need shot lists?

Yes. Solo creators often need shot lists more because they are performing, filming, directing, and editing at the same time. A simple shot list prevents missing shots.

### How long should a shot list be?

It should be as long as the video requires. A 30-second Short may need five to eight shots. A product demo may need ten to twenty. A larger scene may need many more.

### Should shots be listed in story order or shooting order?

Plan the story first, then organize the shoot in the most efficient order. Group shots by setup, location, lighting, and camera angle when possible.

### Can a shot list change during filming?

Yes. A shot list is a plan, not a prison. You can change it when a better idea appears, but you should know what story purpose each change affects.

### What is the biggest shot list mistake?

The biggest mistake is writing visuals without purpose. Every shot should help the viewer understand, feel, believe, or follow something.
