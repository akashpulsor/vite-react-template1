# What Is a Shot List?

![Shot list framework](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/framework.webp)

If you have ever wondered what is a shot list, the simplest answer is this: it is a list of what you need to record so the video can be edited.

That sounds obvious.

But it changes how you shoot.

Without a shot list, you record what is in front of you. With a shot list, you record what the viewer needs to understand.

That is the difference between footage and a video.

Creators often think shot lists are only for film crews. They imagine clipboards, assistant directors, and long days on set. But a solo creator needs a shot list more than a large crew does, because there is no one else to notice what is missing.

The shot list is your quiet second brain.

## What is a shot list for creators?

A creator shot list should not be complicated.

It should answer five questions:

- What is the shot number?
- What is the camera seeing?
- What action happens?
- What sound matters?
- Why does this shot exist?

The last question is the most important.

Here is a useful format:

| Shot | Framing | Action | Sound | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Medium front | Creator looks at messy desk | Room tone | Show problem |
| 2 | Close-up | Hand writes one task | Pen scratch | Make solution concrete |
| 3 | Overhead | Timer starts | Click | Show commitment |
| 4 | Medium side | Creator begins work | Quiet music | Show payoff |

This is small enough to use before shooting.

And that is why it works.

## The mistake: listing visuals without intent

![Good vs bad shot list](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/comparison.webp)

Many shot lists are just nouns.

"Desk shot."

"Close-up."

"B-roll."

Those notes may remind you of something, but they do not tell you what the shot must do.

A useful shot list connects visuals to story.

Bad: "Close-up of coffee."

Better: "Close-up of coffee untouched while creator opens laptop, showing they started before the reward."

Bad: "Shot of shoes."

Better: "Low angle of shoes hitting the floor on the first workout rep."

The difference is not style. It is intention.

When each shot has a job, the edit becomes easier. You stop asking, "What do I have?" and start asking, "Did I capture the moment that proves the point?"

## Turn beats into shots

![Shot list process](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/process.webp)

The easiest way to make a shot list is to start with beats.

A beat is a moment of meaning. A shot is how you show it.

Example beat: "The creator is avoiding work."

Possible shots:

- Medium shot of creator staring at closed laptop.
- Close-up of phone notification.
- Overhead shot of empty notebook.

You do not need all of them. Pick the one that says the most with the least effort.

For Shorts, one beat usually needs one strong shot. For longer videos, one beat may need coverage: wide, medium, close-up, and insert.

But do not add coverage automatically.

Coverage is useful when the edit needs options. It is waste when the idea is already clear.

## Advanced section: how professionals think about coverage

![Professional shot list workflow](/blog/what-is-a-shot-list/advanced.webp)

Professionals do not only list shots. They list edit decisions before shooting.

They think:

- What is the master shot?
- What close-up will hide a cut?
- What insert explains the action?
- What reaction shot carries emotion?
- What sound must be clean?

For a creator, this can be simplified:

- One shot that shows the whole action.
- One shot that shows the important detail.
- One shot that shows the result.

If you are filming a recipe, the whole action may be stirring. The detail may be texture. The result may be the final plate.

If you are filming a talking head, the whole action may be the main explanation. The detail may be a prop or screen. The result may be the before and after.

If you are filming a fitness Short, the whole action may be the movement. The detail may be form. The result may be energy or completion.

That is coverage without waste.

## Complete example: a shot list for a 30 second Short

Short idea: "Make your desk video look more professional with one light move."

Hook: "Your desk video looks flat because your light is too close to the camera."

Shot list:

| Shot | Framing | Action | Sound | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Talking head | Creator points to flat face lighting | Voice | Name the problem |
| 2 | Wide desk | Show light beside camera | Voice | Reveal current setup |
| 3 | Overhead diagram | Draw camera, face, light | Pen sound | Explain the move |
| 4 | Wide desk | Move light 30 degrees sideways | Soft click | Show action |
| 5 | Split compare | Before and after face lighting | Voice | Prove result |
| 6 | Talking head | Creator gives one rule | Voice clear | Make it memorable |

This can be shot in 20 minutes because each shot has a job.

If you miss shot 3, you know the explanation may be confusing. If you miss shot 5, you know the result may not be convincing.

That is why shot lists help.

They make missing pieces visible.

## Final thought

A shot list is not bureaucracy.

It is a way to protect the idea from the chaos of shooting.
