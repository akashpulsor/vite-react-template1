# How to Write Better Hooks for YouTube Shorts

![YouTube Shorts hooks framework](/blog/youtube-shorts-hooks/framework.webp)

Good youtube shorts hooks do not beg for attention. They create a reason to continue.

That difference matters.

Many creators write hooks as if the viewer owes them patience.

"Here are three tips..."

"Today I want to talk about..."

"You won't believe..."

These can work sometimes, but usually they are weak because they ask before they give. A useful hook gives the viewer a small problem, contradiction, or promise in the first second.

The hook is not decoration.

It is the first decision in the video.

## Why creators struggle with YouTube Shorts hooks

Creators often know the topic too well.

Because they know it, they start in the middle. They explain the thing they want to explain, not the thing the viewer is currently feeling.

The viewer is not thinking, "Please teach me your framework."

The viewer is thinking something smaller:

- "Why do my videos feel boring?"
- "Why does my room look cheap on camera?"
- "Why do I keep restarting this habit?"
- "Why does my audio sound worse than other creators?"

A hook works when it meets the viewer at that small, specific thought.

Here is the basic test:

| Weak hook | Better hook |
|---|---|
| "Here are lighting tips." | "Your face looks flat because your light is in the wrong place." |
| "Let me show you my routine." | "I stopped checking my phone before work by changing one object on my desk." |
| "Three editing mistakes." | "This cut makes your Short feel slower even when it is faster." |

The better hook is not louder.

It is more precise.

## The four-part hook framework

![Good vs bad YouTube Shorts hooks](/blog/youtube-shorts-hooks/comparison.webp)

Use four questions:

- What pain does the viewer recognize?
- What surprise makes them curious?
- What proof will they see soon?
- What payoff will they get by staying?

You do not need all four in one sentence. But the first two seconds should contain at least two.

Example:

"Your talking head video looks amateur because the background is brighter than your face."

Pain: the video looks amateur.

Surprise: the problem is not the camera.

Proof: the viewer expects to see a lighting setup.

Payoff: they can fix the shot.

That is enough.

Another example:

"If your Short feels slow, remove the first sentence."

Pain: the Short feels slow.

Surprise: the fix is removing, not adding.

Proof: the creator can show before and after.

Payoff: a faster opening.

The hook creates a little machine in the viewer's head. It asks a question and promises not to waste time answering it.

## Write the first frame and the first line together

![Hook writing process](/blog/youtube-shorts-hooks/process.webp)

For Shorts, the first frame is part of the hook.

A strong line with a dead first frame still feels weak. A strong first frame with a vague line feels confusing.

Pair them.

| First line | First frame |
|---|---|
| "This is why your desk videos look flat." | Creator beside a flat, bright wall |
| "Do not start your Short with context." | Timeline showing first sentence cut out |
| "Your phone camera is good enough. Your setup is not." | Phone on tripod, messy background |

The first frame should make the line easier to believe.

If the line says "your background is too bright," show the background. If the line says "your hook is too slow," show the old hook being crossed out. If the line says "this shot list saves time," show the list.

Do not hide the evidence.

## Advanced section: hook diagnosis

![Professional hook workflow](/blog/youtube-shorts-hooks/advanced.webp)

Professionals do not ask, "Is this hook catchy?"

They ask:

- Does it name a real viewer state?
- Does it create a question?
- Does the next shot answer that question?
- Does it avoid false urgency?
- Can it be understood without context?

False urgency is common in Shorts.

"Stop doing this now" is urgency.

But if the advice is small, the urgency feels fake.

It is better to be calm and specific.

"Move your key light 30 degrees to the side" may sound less dramatic, but it gives the viewer a concrete reason to watch.

The strongest hooks often feel almost plain.

They win because they are useful.

## Complete example: rewriting hooks

Topic: better phone video quality.

Bad hook: "Here are five ways to make your videos better."

Problem: too broad, no tension, no visual proof.

Rewrite 1: "Your phone footage looks cheap because your room is doing too much."

This is better. It names a hidden cause.

Rewrite 2: "Before buying a camera, fix these three things in your room."

This is also better. It creates a money-saving promise.

Rewrite 3: "Your phone camera is fine. Your background, light, and audio are fighting it."

This is probably the best for an educational Short. It gives a clear structure and sets up three visual beats.

Shot plan:

- Show phone footage that looks messy.
- Point to the bright background.
- Move the light.
- Move the microphone closer.
- Show the same phone shot again.

The hook is now connected to the video. That is the whole point.

## Final thought

A hook is not a trick.

It is a small act of respect.

It tells the viewer, quickly, why this video exists and what question it will answer.
