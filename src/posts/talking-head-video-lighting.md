# How to Light a Talking Head Video

![Talking head video lighting framework](/blog/talking-head-video-lighting/framework.webp)

Good talking head video lighting is not about owning expensive lights. It is about deciding what the viewer should look at first.

Usually that should be the face.

Many creator videos look amateur because the brightest thing in the frame is not the person. It is a wall, a window, a monitor, or a white table. The camera exposes for the wrong thing, and the face becomes dull.

Lighting is attention control.

Once you see it that way, the setup becomes simpler.

## Talking head video lighting starts with the key light

The key light is the main light on the face.

Place it slightly above eye level and about 30 to 45 degrees to one side of the camera.

Not directly above.

Not directly beside the face.

Not attached to the camera unless you want a flat look.

The side placement gives the face shape. One side is brighter. The other side has gentle shadow. That small difference makes the image feel less like a webcam.

Use this simple test:

| If the face looks... | Try this |
|---|---|
| Flat | Move key light to the side |
| Too harsh | Increase light size or bounce it |
| Too dark on one side | Add weak fill or move key closer to camera |
| Separated poorly from background | Add rim light or darken background |

The goal is not drama for its own sake.

The goal is clarity with depth.

## The common mistakes

![Good vs bad talking head lighting](/blog/talking-head-video-lighting/comparison.webp)

The most common mistake is using a bright window behind you.

The second is putting the face in the same brightness as the background.

The third is adding too many lights before controlling one.

Start with one light.

Turn off the others.

Move the key light until the face looks good. Then decide what is missing.

If the shadow side is too dark, use bounce. A white wall, foam board, or even a light cloth can reflect a little light back. Bounce is softer than adding another direct light.

If the background is too bright, darken it. Move away from the wall. Turn off the background monitor. Close the curtain slightly.

If the person blends into the background, add a rim light behind and to the side. It should be subtle. If it looks like a glowing outline, it is too strong.

## A simple creator lighting process

![Talking head lighting process](/blog/talking-head-video-lighting/process.webp)

Use this order:

- Place the camera first.
- Place the person second.
- Set the background third.
- Add the key light.
- Add bounce or negative fill.
- Add rim light only if needed.
- Check the eyes.

The eyes matter because they tell you whether the person feels alive on camera. A small catchlight is useful. Dead eyes make even good cameras feel flat.

Negative fill is also worth understanding.

It means taking light away. If one side of the face is too bright, place something dark on that side: a black cloth, a dark board, even an open doorway. This creates contrast without adding a light.

Most creators think lighting means adding.

Often it means removing.

## Advanced section: how professionals think about contrast

![Professional talking head lighting workflow](/blog/talking-head-video-lighting/advanced.webp)

Professionals think in ratios.

Not math-heavy ratios on every shoot, but visual relationships:

- Face brighter than background.
- Key side brighter than shadow side.
- Eyes visible.
- Background separated.
- No random object brighter than the subject.

A polished talking head video often has:

- Soft key light.
- Controlled shadow side.
- Slight background falloff.
- Small rim or hair light.
- Clean audio.

Lighting and audio are connected because both create trust. If the viewer can see the face and hear the voice clearly, the video feels more intentional even if the room is ordinary.

## Complete example: one-room setup

You have:

- A phone.
- A window.
- One LED light.
- A white wall.
- A desk lamp.

Setup:

- Put the window to the side, not behind you.
- Use the LED as key light if the window changes too much.
- Move yourself 4 to 6 feet from the wall.
- Turn on the desk lamp in the background for depth.
- Use a white board on the shadow side if the face is too contrasty.
- Turn down screen brightness if the monitor pulls attention.

Shot check:

- Is the face the brightest meaningful object?
- Is the background quieter than the face?
- Do the eyes have life?
- Is one side of the face gently shaped?
- Is the voice clear?

If yes, shoot.

Do not keep adjusting forever. Lighting is there to help the idea, not replace it.

## Final thought

Good talking head lighting is mostly judgment.

Place the viewer's attention where it belongs. Remove distractions. Give the face shape.
